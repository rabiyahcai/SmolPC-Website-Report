Partner Introduction

Project Partner

SmolPC was developed under the supervision of Professor Dean Mohamedally at UCL's Department of Computer Science. He acted as the primary academic stakeholder for the project, helping define the educational problem, review major design decisions, and keep the work aligned with practical classroom use rather than a purely technical demonstration.

Educational Stakeholders

Requirements were also shaped by discussions with teachers and by external demonstrations of the project. These conversations consistently pushed the platform towards privacy, ease of deployment, practical classroom value, and support for everyday student workflows rather than isolated AI features.

Software Context

The final platform was designed around tasks students and teachers regularly perform across coding, writing, image editing, and 3D work. This is why the scope expanded beyond the original LibreOffice prototype to include CodeHelper together with assistants for LibreOffice, GIMP, and Blender inside one unified local platform.

Project Background

Artificial intelligence is transforming education, yet access to AI-powered learning tools remains deeply unequal. Most mainstream systems still depend on cloud accounts, reliable internet connectivity, and platform lock-in, which makes them difficult to deploy in privacy-sensitive or under-resourced educational settings. According to UNESCO, the majority of primary schools and a significant proportion of secondary schools worldwide lack internet connectivity. [1] At the same time, teachers are under increasing workload pressure [2], and schools risk falling further behind in the emerging AI divide. [3]

The project began from an earlier SmolPC LibreOffice prototype that demonstrated local AI assistance was possible, but also exposed important limitations. It centred on one application, relied on a more cumbersome local inference setup, and did not translate cleanly into a broader classroom platform. As the project scope expanded to include CodeHelper, GIMP, and Blender, it became clear that simply extending the old architecture would create repeated deployment, security, and maintainability problems.

Those constraints shaped the final direction of SmolPC 2.0. Rather than remaining a LibreOffice-first prototype, the project evolved into a unified offline AI platform with CodeHelper as the flagship experience and shared support for writing, presentations, image editing, and 3D workflows. This shift also changed the technical requirements: cross-platform desktop development, local privacy, easier packaging, reduced setup burden, and hardware-aware local inference became core project goals rather than secondary concerns.

Project Goals

The overarching goal of SmolPC is to deliver a free, offline, open-source AI platform for education that makes local AI practical to deploy and use across coding, writing, image editing, and 3D design, with CodeHelper as the flagship experience inside a broader unified system.

Specifically, SmolPC aims to:

  • Provide a unified desktop experience that brings together CodeHelper and assistants for LibreOffice, GIMP, and Blender.
  • Enable students and teachers to interact with software through natural language, lowering the barrier to coding, writing, editing, and creative work.
  • Keep prompts, generated content, and inference local to the device during normal use, supporting privacy-sensitive educational deployment.
  • Reduce deployment friction by avoiding workflows that require students or teachers to manage a separate model server or run ad hoc setup commands.
  • Use hardware-aware local inference so the platform can choose the best supported runtime path for the device it is installed on.
  • Maintain an extensible architecture so additional assistants or workflows can be added without rebuilding the platform from scratch.

Gathering Requirements

Requirements were gathered iteratively rather than fixed once at the start. Because SmolPC evolved through several architectural pivots during development, the final requirements reflect both early stakeholder goals and constraints discovered through implementation.

  1. Legacy baseline: we reviewed the original SmolPC LibreOffice and Ollama-based prototype to identify what was reusable and what prevented broader adoption.
  2. Scope expansion: new project objectives introduced CodeHelper together with assistants for GIMP and Blender, turning the project from a single-application prototype into a multi-workflow platform.
  3. Prototype-driven refinement: early assistant development exposed problems around cross-platform development, duplicated inference logic, security boundaries, and the deployment overhead of relying on a separately managed model server.
  4. Deployment-driven refinement: as the goal shifted towards realistic school deployment, requirements expanded to include a unified app, a shared local engine, hardware-aware runtime selection, and a setup flow that avoids asking students or teachers to manage local inference infrastructure manually.

We did not rely on a formal pupil survey. Direct access to school-age users was limited for safeguarding reasons, so requirements were refined through supervisor discussions, teacher-facing feedback, prototype evaluation, and iterative internal testing of realistic workflows.

We also conducted a comparative analysis of cloud AI tools against local deployment goals. The comparison reinforced that the decisive requirements were not only AI capability, but privacy, deployability, local control, and practical classroom use.

Personas

Based on the information we've collected, we constructed the following personas:

Persona of a Student

A secondary-school student who uses a school or personal laptop for programming, coursework, presentations, and creative assignments. They are comfortable asking questions in plain English, but not configuring local AI tooling or debugging installation problems. They need a tool that feels approachable, keeps their work private, and helps across more than one subject area.

Persona of a Teacher

A teacher or teaching assistant who prepares lesson material, supports students in computing and digital media work, and values predictable deployment. They want useful AI support for classroom tasks, but cannot depend on cloud services, paid subscriptions, or setup steps that would be difficult to repeat across many student devices.

Use Case List

The following use cases describe how students and teachers interact with SmolPC v2.0 as a unified local AI platform. Each use case maps to one or more functional requirements listed in the MoSCoW section below.

UC1 — Start SmolPC and Send a Text Request

ActorStudent / Teacher
Pre-conditionSmolPC is installed and the shared local engine is ready or can be started from the application.
Main Flow
  1. User opens SmolPC and selects an assistant mode.
  2. User types a request in the chat box.
  3. The request is routed through the shared local engine and the tool provider for the active mode.
  4. A loading or readiness indicator is shown while processing.
  5. The response appears in the workspace and, where relevant, triggers the requested tool action.
Post-conditionThe response is visible in the active workspace and the request has been handled locally.
ExceptionIf the runtime or host application is unavailable, the user is shown setup guidance or a clear recoverable error.

UC2 — Get Coding Help from CodeHelper

ActorStudent
Pre-conditionCode mode is selected in SmolPC and the local model is ready.
Main Flow
  1. Student types a coding question or pastes code into the chat.
  2. Student optionally uses a prompt starter or follow-up question.
  3. CodeHelper returns an explanation, debugging help, or generated code.
  4. Student continues the conversation until the problem is understood or solved.
Post-conditionStudent receives a locally generated coding answer suited to the task.

UC3 — Create or Edit a Document in LibreOffice Writer

ActorStudent / Teacher
Pre-conditionLibreOffice is installed and Writer mode is available in SmolPC.
Main Flow
  1. User opens Writer mode from the unified application.
  2. User creates a new document or works on an existing one.
  3. User asks the assistant to draft, edit, structure, or format content.
  4. The Writer workflow applies the requested actions and returns a summary.
  5. User reviews the result and requests further refinement if needed.
Post-conditionThe document is updated locally according to the request.

UC4 — Create or Edit a Presentation in Slides

ActorTeacher / Student
Pre-conditionLibreOffice is installed and Slides mode is available in SmolPC.
Main Flow
  1. User opens Slides mode from the unified application.
  2. User asks for a new presentation, new slide content, or edits to an existing deck.
  3. The assistant generates slide-level actions and applies them through the LibreOffice workflow.
  4. User reviews the updated deck and continues refining it in natural language.
Post-conditionThe presentation is created or updated locally according to the request.

UC5 — Edit an Image in GIMP

ActorStudent / Teacher
Pre-conditionGIMP is installed, GIMP mode is available, and an image is open or ready to be edited.
Main Flow
  1. User switches to GIMP mode.
  2. User asks for an edit such as cropping, blurring, adjusting colour, or applying a transformation.
  3. The GIMP workflow maps the request to local tool actions.
  4. The image is updated and the user asks for follow-up changes if needed.
Post-conditionThe requested image edit is completed locally inside the GIMP workflow.

UC6 — Get Scene-Aware Help in Blender

ActorStudent
Pre-conditionBlender is installed, Blender mode is available, and a Blender scene is open.
Main Flow
  1. User switches to Blender mode.
  2. User asks a question about the current scene, object, material, or workflow.
  3. The assistant uses local context and retrieval to produce a scene-aware answer.
  4. User follows the guidance and continues the conversation with follow-up questions.
Post-conditionUser receives Blender help that is grounded in the local workflow rather than a generic web-style answer.

UC7 — Switch Modes and Continue Previous Work

ActorStudent / Teacher
Pre-conditionAt least one previous conversation exists locally and the unified application is open.
Main Flow
  1. User opens the mode selector or sidebar.
  2. User switches from one workflow to another, for example from CodeHelper to Writer or Blender.
  3. User optionally opens a previous conversation associated with that mode.
  4. The conversation loads and the user continues from the previous state.
Post-conditionMode-specific work and chat history remain available inside one unified application.

MoSCoW Requirements

Requirements were prioritised using the MoSCoW method, separating what the system must deliver from what would be beneficial but is not critical. The list below reflects the final agreed requirements following stakeholder interviews and pseudo-user testing.

Functional Requirements

ID Requirement Priority
FR-M1 The platform shall accept natural language requests via text input across all assistant modes. Must Have
FR-M2 The platform shall provide a unified desktop shell that brings together CodeHelper and assistant workflows for LibreOffice, GIMP, and Blender. Must Have
FR-M3 CodeHelper shall answer coding questions, explain programming concepts, and generate or debug code in plain language. Must Have
FR-M4 The LibreOffice assistant shall support document and presentation workflows through natural language requests. Must Have
FR-M5 The GIMP assistant shall support image-editing workflows through natural language requests. Must Have
FR-M6 The Blender assistant shall support scene-aware 3D help and workflow guidance through natural language requests. Must Have
FR-M7 All AI inference shall run locally on-device during normal use, with no cloud dependency after setup. Must Have
FR-M8 User prompts, generated content, and assistant interactions shall remain on-device during normal use. Must Have
FR-M9 The platform shall provide a deployment path that does not require students or teachers to manually install or operate a separate local model server. Must Have
FR-S1 The system shall display a visible loading indicator while the AI model is processing a request. Should Have
FR-S2 The system shall persist chat history per tool across sessions so users can continue previous conversations. Should Have
FR-S3 The platform shall detect required host applications and provide setup guidance when a workflow is unavailable. Should Have
FR-S4 The platform shall choose the best supported local runtime path for the current machine and fall back gracefully when a preferred accelerator is unavailable. Should Have
FR-S5 The unified app shall allow users to open or focus the relevant host application directly from the active workflow. Should Have
FR-S6 The system shall allow users to recover from missing prerequisites or runtime failures without losing chat state. Should Have
FR-C1 The platform could include local benchmark or diagnostics features to help compare runtime behaviour on different machines. Could Have
FR-C2 A local retrieval-augmented workflow could surface relevant documentation or help content for supported tools. Could Have
FR-C3 The UI could offer a high-contrast mode and dyslexia-friendly font options to further support users with accessibility needs. Could Have
FR-W1 The system will not use cloud-hosted AI models or transmit any user data to external servers. Won't Have
